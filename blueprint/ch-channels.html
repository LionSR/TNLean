Quantum Channels and Positive Maps

This chapter develops the theory of positive maps and quantum channels on matrix algebras, following  [ Wol12 ] .

Positive maps and channels

Definition .1 Positive map

IsPositiveMap A linear map \(E : M_{D}(\mathbb {C}) \to M_{D}(\mathbb {C})\) is positive if \(E(X) \ge 0\) whenever \(X \ge 0\), where \(X \ge 0\) means that \(X\) is positive semidefinite.

Remark .2

We use the Loewner order on matrices: \(X \le Y\) means \(Y - X \ge 0\).

Definition .3 Trace-preserving map

IsTracePreservingMap A linear map \(E : M_{D}(\mathbb {C}) \to M_{D}(\mathbb {C})\) is trace-preserving if \(\tr (E(X)) = \tr (X)\) for all \(X\).

Definition .4 Completely positive map

IsCPMap A linear map \(E : M_{D}(\mathbb {C}) \to M_{D}(\mathbb {C})\) is completely positive (CP) if it admits a Kraus representation: there exist operators \(\{ K_i\} _{i=0}^{r-1}\) with \(K_i \in M_{D}(\mathbb {C})\) such that, for every \(X \in M_{D}(\mathbb {C})\),

\[ E(X) = \sum _{i=0}^{r-1} K_i X K_i^\dagger . \]

We use the Kraus characterisation because it matches the later Kadison–Schwarz arguments. By Choi’s theorem, this is equivalent to \(E \otimes \id _n\) being positive for all \(n\).

Theorem .5 CP maps are positive

IsCPMap.isPositiveMap def:cp_map, def:positive_map Every completely positive map is positive.

▼
If \(X \ge 0\), then each summand \(K_i X K_i^\dagger \ge 0\) (conjugation preserves positive semidefiniteness), so the sum is PSD.
▼

Theorem .6 Channels are positive

IsChannel.pos def:channel, def:positive_map Every quantum channel is positive.

▼
thm:cp_is_positive A quantum channel is completely positive by definition, so Theorem .5 applies.
▼

Theorem .7 Positive maps preserve Hermiticity

IsPositiveMap.map_isHermitian def:positive_map If \(E\) is positive and \(X = X^\dagger \), then \(E(X) = E(X)^\dagger \).

▼
Decompose \(X = X_+ - X_-\) into its positive and negative parts. Positivity makes \(E(X_+)\) and \(E(X_-)\) positive semidefinite, hence Hermitian, so their difference \(E(X)\) is Hermitian.
▼

Definition .8 Quantum channel

IsChannel def:cp_map, def:trace_preserving A linear map \(E : M_{D}(\mathbb {C}) \to M_{D}(\mathbb {C})\) is a quantum channel if it is both completely positive and trace-preserving (CPTP), following  [ Wol12 ] .

Definition .9 Density matrices

densityMatrices, mem_densityMatrices The set of density matrices in \(M_{D}(\mathbb {C})\) is

\[ \mathcal{D}_D = \{ \rho \in M_{D}(\mathbb {C}) : \rho \ge 0, \tr (\rho ) = 1\} . \]

Theorem .10 Density matrices are compact

densityMatrices_isCompact def:density_matrices \(\mathcal{D}_D\) is compact (in the entry-wise topology on \(M_{D}(\mathbb {C})\)).

▼
The PSD cone is closed (preimage of the closed non-negative cone under continuous quadratic forms), and if \(\rho \ge 0\) with \(\tr (\rho ) = 1\) then each entry satisfies \(|\rho _{ij}|^2 \le \rho _{ii}\rho _{jj} \le 1\): the diagonal entries are non-negative and sum to \(1\), and the off-diagonal bound is the PSD Cauchy–Schwarz inequality. Hence the matrix entries are uniformly bounded, and Heine–Borel gives compactness.
▼

Theorem .11 Density matrices are convex

densityMatrices_isConvex def:density_matrices \(\mathcal{D}_D\) is convex.

▼
Convex combination of PSD matrices is PSD, and trace is linear.
▼

Theorem .12 Density matrices are nonempty

densityMatrices_nonempty def:density_matrices For \(D \ge 1\), \(\mathcal{D}_D \neq \varnothing \).

▼
\(\frac{1}{D} \mathbb {1}_D\) is a density matrix.
▼

Theorem .13 Channels preserve density matrices

IsChannel.map_densityMatrices def:channel, def:density_matrices If \(E\) is a quantum channel, then \(E(\mathcal{D}_D) \subseteq \mathcal{D}_D\).

▼
thm:cp_is_positive Complete positivity implies positivity (Theorem .5), so \(E(\rho ) \ge 0\); trace preservation gives \(\tr (E(\rho )) = \tr (\rho ) = 1\).
▼

Irreducibility

Definition .14 Orthogonal projection

IsOrthogonalProjection A matrix \(P \in M_{D}(\mathbb {C})\) is an orthogonal projection if \(P = P^\dagger \) and \(P^2 = P\).

Definition .15 Irreducible map

IsIrreducibleMap def:orthogonal_projection_channel A linear map \(E : M_{D}(\mathbb {C}) \to M_{D}(\mathbb {C})\) is irreducible if whenever \(P\) is an orthogonal projection satisfying \(E(P M_{D}(\mathbb {C}) P) \subseteq P M_{D}(\mathbb {C}) P\), then \(P = 0\) or \(P = \mathbb {1}\). This is [ Wol12 , Theorem 6.2, condition (1) ] . The definition applies to any linear map; complete positivity is not required.

Theorem .16 Injectivity implies irreducibility

MPSTensor.injective_implies_irreducibleCP def:injective, def:transfer_map, def:irreducible_cp If \(A\) is an injective MPS tensor, then its transfer map \(\mathcal{E}_A\) is irreducible.

▼
If \(P\) is an invariant projection for \(\mathcal{E}_A\), then \((\mathbb {1}- P) A^i P = 0\) for all \(i\). Since the \(\{ A^i\} \) span \(M_{D}(\mathbb {C})\), \((\mathbb {1}- P) M P = 0\) for all \(M\), so \(P = 0\) or \(P = \mathbb {1}\).
▼

Remark .17 Transfer map is CP

MPSTensor.transferMap_isCPMap def:transfer_map, def:cp_map The transfer map \(\mathcal{E}_A(X) = \sum _i A^i X (A^i)^\dagger \) is completely positive: it is already written in Kraus form, with Kraus operators \(\{ A^i\} \).

Theorem .18 Transfer map is a channel

MPSTensor.transferMap_isChannel def:transfer_map, def:channel If \(\sum _i (A^i)^\dagger A^i = \mathbb {1}\), then the transfer map \(\mathcal{E}_A(X) = \sum _i A^i X (A^i)^\dagger \) is a quantum channel (CPTP).

▼
thm:transfer_cp Complete positivity is the observation of Remark .17. Trace preservation: \(\tr (\mathcal{E}_A(X)) = \sum _i \tr (A^i X (A^i)^\dagger ) = \tr ((\sum _i (A^i)^\dagger A^i) X) = \tr (X)\) by cyclicity and the normalization hypothesis.
▼

Cesàro fixed-point theorem

Definition .19 Cesàro mean

cesaroMean The Cesàro mean of a linear map \(E\) at order \(N\), applied to a starting matrix \(\rho _0\), is

\[ \sigma _N = \frac{1}{N} \sum _{n=0}^{N-1} E^n(\rho _0). \]

Theorem .20 Cesàro telescope

cesaroMean_telescope def:cesaro_mean \(E(\sigma _N) - \sigma _N = \frac{1}{N}(E^N(\rho _0) - \rho _0)\).

▼
def:cesaro_mean Telescoping: \(E(\sigma _N) = \frac{1}{N}\sum _{n=1}^{N} E^n(\rho _0)\) and subtracting \(\sigma _N\) cancels all but the first and last terms.
▼

Theorem .21 Hermitian fixed-point decomposition

IsChannel.posSemidef_parts_of_hermitian_fixedPoint def:channel Let \(E\) be a quantum channel and \(X = E(X)\) a Hermitian fixed point with decomposition \(X = P_+ - P_-\) into orthogonal positive and negative parts (\(P_\pm \ge 0\), \(\tr (P_+ P_-) = 0\)). Then \(E(P_+) = P_+\) and \(E(P_-) = P_-\). This is [ Wol12 , Proposition 6.8 ] .

▼
Let \(Q\) be the support projection of \(P_+\). Trace preservation and positivity give \(\tr (Q E(P_-)) = 0\) and \(E(P_-) \ge 0\), so \(Q E(P_-) = 0\). The fixed-point equation \(E(P_+) - E(P_-) = P_+ - P_-\) then identifies \(E(P_\pm ) = P_\pm \).
▼

Theorem .22 Cesàro fixed-point theorem

IsChannel.exists_posSemidef_fixedPoint def:channel, def:density_matrices, def:cesaro_mean Every quantum channel \(E : M_{D}(\mathbb {C}) \to M_{D}(\mathbb {C})\) (with \(D \ge 1\)) has a nonzero positive semidefinite fixed point: there exists \(\rho \ge 0\), \(\rho \neq 0\), with \(E(\rho ) = \rho \).

▼
thm:density_compact, thm:density_convex, thm:density_nonempty, thm:channel_preserves_density, thm:cesaro_telescope Start with any \(\rho _0 \in \mathcal{D}_D\) (Theorem .12). Each Cesàro mean \(\sigma _N\) lies in \(\mathcal{D}_D\) (by convexity and channel preservation). By compactness (Theorem .10), extract a convergent subsequence \(\sigma _{N_k} \to \rho \). The telescope identity (Theorem .20) gives \(\| E(\sigma _N) - \sigma _N\| = \frac{1}{N}\| E^N(\rho _0) - \rho _0\| \to 0\), so \(E(\rho ) = \rho \) by continuity. In  [ Wol12 , Theorem 6.11 ] , existence is proved via Brouwer’s fixed-point theorem; we use the Cesàro argument instead.
▼

Fixed-point projection

Definition .23 Fixed-point projection

fixedPointProj Given a linear map \(E : M_{D}(\mathbb {C}) \to M_{D}(\mathbb {C})\) and a fixed point \(\rho \) with \(E(\rho ) = \rho \) and \(\tr (\rho ) \neq 0\), the fixed-point projection is the rank-one map

\[ P(X) = \frac{\tr (X)}{\tr (\rho )} \rho . \]

This projects onto the span of \(\rho \) along the kernel of the trace functional. When the fixed point is unique up to scaling, this span is the full fixed-point space.

Theorem .24 Power decomposition

pow_eq_fixedPointProj_add_compl_pow def:fixed_point_proj, def:trace_preserving If \(E\) is trace-preserving and \(E(\rho ) = \rho \), then for \(n \ge 1\),

\[ E^n = P + (E - P)^n, \]

where \(P\) is the fixed-point projection.

▼
def:fixed_point_proj \(P\) is idempotent (\(P^2 = P\)), the fixed-point relation \(E(\rho ) = \rho \) gives \(E \circ P = P\), and trace preservation gives \(P \circ E = P\). These imply \((E-P) \circ P = 0\) and \(P \circ (E-P) = 0\), so in the binomial expansion of \((P + (E-P))^n\) all cross terms vanish, leaving \(E^n = P^n + (E-P)^n = P + (E-P)^n\).
▼

Peripheral spectrum and primitivity

Definition .25 Peripheral spectrum

peripheralSpectrum The peripheral spectrum of a bounded linear operator \(T\) is the set of spectral values \(\mu \) with \(|\mu |\) equal to the spectral radius of \(T\).

Definition .26 Peripheral eigenvalues

peripheralEigenvalues The peripheral eigenvalues of a linear map \(E\) on a finite-dimensional space are the eigenvalues \(\lambda \) on the unit circle: \(|\lambda | = 1\). For a channel, the spectral radius is \(1\) [ Wol12 , Proposition 6.1 ] , so these coincide with the eigenvalues of maximal modulus.

The condition \(|\lambda | = 1\) is appropriate for channels, since the spectral radius of a channel is \(1\); for a general linear map with spectral radius \(r \neq 1\) the condition would generalise to \(|\lambda | = r\).

Theorem .27 \(1\) is a peripheral eigenvalue

one_mem_peripheralEigenvalues def:peripheral_eigenvalues If \(E\) has a nonzero fixed point \(\rho \neq 0\) with \(E(\rho ) = \rho \), then \(1\) is a peripheral eigenvalue of \(E\).

▼
\(\rho \) is an eigenvector with eigenvalue \(1\), and \(|1| = 1\).
▼

Remark .28 Peripheral eigenvalues lie on the unit circle

peripheralEigenvalues_subset_unit_circle def:peripheral_eigenvalues Every peripheral eigenvalue \(\lambda \) satisfies \(|\lambda | = 1\). This is simply the defining condition in Definition .26.

Theorem .29 Finiteness of peripheral eigenvalues

peripheralEigenvalues_finite def:peripheral_eigenvalues On a finite-dimensional space, the set of peripheral eigenvalues is finite.

▼
Peripheral eigenvalues are a subset of all eigenvalues, which is a finite set in finite dimensions.
▼

Theorem .30 Power-stable peripheral eigenvalues are roots of unity

peripheral_isRootOfUnity_of_pow_eigenvalue def:peripheral_eigenvalues Let \(E\) be a linear endomorphism on a finite-dimensional space. If \(\mu \) is a peripheral eigenvalue of \(E\) and \(\mu ^n\) is an eigenvalue of \(E\) for every \(n \ge 1\), then \(\mu \) is a root of unity.

▼
Since \(\mu ^n\) is an eigenvalue for every \(n\), the pigeonhole principle on the finite eigenvalue set gives \(\mu ^a = \mu ^b\) for some \(a \neq b\), hence \(\mu ^{|a-b|} = 1\).
▼

Theorem .31 Peripheral powers imply root of unity

peripheral_isRootOfUnity_of_closed_powers def:peripheral_eigenvalues If the set of peripheral eigenvalues of a linear endomorphism \(E\) on a finite-dimensional space is closed under powers (\(\mu \in \mathrm{peripheral}(E)\) and \(n \ge 1\) imply \(\mu ^n \in \mathrm{peripheral}(E)\)), then every peripheral eigenvalue is a root of unity.

▼
thm:peripheral_roots_of_unity The closure hypothesis provides \(\mu ^n \in \mathrm{peripheral}(E)\) for all \(n\), which in particular means \(\mu ^n\) is an eigenvalue. Theorem .30 then gives \(\mu ^p = 1\) for some \(p {\gt} 0\).
▼

Periodicity removal by powering

Remark .32

The results in this subsection are purely spectral: they use only finiteness of a set of complex numbers and the spectral mapping theorem for powers. In particular, they do not rely on complete positivity. For transfer maps, replacing a tensor by its \(p\)-blocked tensor replaces \(\mathcal{E}_A\) by \(\mathcal{E}_A^p\), so this powering step is the operator-theoretic form of blocking.

Lemma .33 Common exponent for a finite set of roots of unity

exists_common_power_eq_one_of_finite Let \(s \subseteq \mathbb {C}\) be a finite set. Assume that for every \(\mu \in s\) there exists an exponent \(p_\mu {\gt} 0\) with \(\mu ^{p_\mu } = 1\). Then there exists \(p {\gt} 0\) such that \(\mu ^p = 1\) for all \(\mu \in s\).

▼
Take \(p\) to be the least common multiple of the exponents \(p_\mu \).
▼

Lemma .34 Powering collapses peripheral eigenvalues

peripheralEigenvalues_pow_eq_singleton def:peripheral_eigenvalues Let \(E : M_{D}(\mathbb {C}) \to M_{D}(\mathbb {C})\) be a linear map, and assume that \(E\) has a nonzero fixed point \(\rho \neq 0\). Let \(p {\gt} 0\). If every peripheral eigenvalue \(\mu \) of \(E\) satisfies \(\mu ^p = 1\), then \(\mathrm{peripheral}(E^p) = \{ 1\} \).

▼
thm:one_peripheral The fixed point \(\rho \) gives \(1 \in \mathrm{peripheral}(E^p)\). For the reverse inclusion, let \(\nu \in \mathrm{peripheral}(E^p)\). Spectral mapping gives \(\nu = \mu ^p\) for some \(\mu \in \sigma (E)\). From \(|\nu | = 1\) and \(p {\gt} 0\) we obtain \(|\mu | = 1\), hence \(\mu \in \mathrm{peripheral}(E)\). The hypothesis \(\mu ^p = 1\) then forces \(\nu = 1\).
▼

For a normalized MPS tensor, the transfer map is naturally trace-preserving but need not be unital. In general one should not expect a single gauge choice to make it both unital and trace-preserving.

Remark .35 Bi-canonical special case

When a Kraus map happens to be both unital and trace-preserving (the “bi-canonical” case), the Kadison–Schwarz equality at peripheral eigenvectors (Theorem .41) shows directly that the peripheral eigenvalues are closed under powers, and hence are roots of unity by Theorem .31. This is the simplest argument, but it requires both normalizations simultaneously. The more general adjoint-fixed-point formulation below covers the case where only unitality and a positive definite adjoint fixed point are available.

Kadison–Schwarz input for peripheral and fixed-point arguments

Definition .36 Kraus maps, adjoints, and normalizations

KadisonSchwarz.krausMap, KadisonSchwarz.krausAdjointMap, KadisonSchwarz.IsUnitalKraus, KadisonSchwarz.IsTPKraus For operators \(\{ K_i\} _{i=0}^{d-1} \subseteq M_{D}(\mathbb {C})\), the associated Kraus map and adjoint Kraus map are

\[ E(X) = \sum _i K_i X K_i^\dagger , \qquad E^*(X) = \sum _i K_i^\dagger X K_i. \]

The Kraus family is unital when \(\sum _i K_i K_i^\dagger = \mathbb {1}\) and trace-preserving when \(\sum _i K_i^\dagger K_i = \mathbb {1}\).

Theorem .37 Kadison–Schwarz inequality

KadisonSchwarz.kadison_schwarz def:kraus_map_channels Let \(E(X) = \sum _i K_i X K_i^\dagger \) be a unital Kraus map. Then for every \(X \in M_{D}(\mathbb {C})\),

\[ E(X^\dagger X) \ge E(X)^\dagger E(X). \]

This is [ Wol12 , Equation (5.2) ] .

▼
Apply the unital completely positive map entrywise to the positive \(2 \times 2\) block matrix \(\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix} \end{smallmatrix} X^\dagger X & X^\dagger \\ X & \mathbb {1}\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)\) and take the Schur complement of the lower-right block.
▼

Theorem .38 Hilbert–Schmidt contraction

KadisonSchwarz.hilbertSchmidt_contraction def:kraus_map_channels, thm:kadison_schwarz_channels If a Kraus map is both unital and trace-preserving, then

\[ \tr (E(X)^\dagger E(X)) \le \tr (X^\dagger X) \]

for all \(X \in M_{D}(\mathbb {C})\).

▼
thm:kadison_schwarz_channels The Kadison–Schwarz gap is positive semidefinite by Theorem .37. Trace preservation then identifies the traces of its two terms.
▼

Theorem .39 KS gap decomposition

KadisonSchwarz.ks_gap_eq_sum_squares def:kraus_map_channels For a unital Kraus map,

\[ E(X^\dagger X) - E(X)^\dagger E(X) = \sum _i R_i^\dagger R_i, \qquad R_i := X K_i^\dagger - K_i^\dagger E(X). \]

▼
Expand the right-hand side, distribute the products, and use \(\sum _i K_i K_i^\dagger = \mathbb {1}\) to recover the Kadison–Schwarz gap.
▼

Theorem .40 KS equality implies Kraus commutation

KadisonSchwarz.kraus_commute_of_ks_equality thm:ks_gap_decomp_channels Let \(E\) be a unital Kraus map. If \(E(X^\dagger X) = E(X)^\dagger E(X)\), then, for every \(i\),

\[ X K_i^\dagger = K_i^\dagger E(X). \]

▼
thm:ks_gap_decomp_channels The Kadison–Schwarz gap is a sum of positive semidefinite terms \(\sum _i R_i^\dagger R_i\). If the gap vanishes, each \(R_i\) must vanish.
▼

Theorem .41 KS equality for peripheral eigenvectors

KadisonSchwarz.ks_equality_of_peripheral_eigenvector def:kraus_map_channels, thm:kadison_schwarz_channels Let \(E\) be a Kraus map that is both unital and trace-preserving. If \(E(X) = \mu X\) with \(|\mu | = 1\), then

\[ E(X^\dagger X) = E(X)^\dagger E(X). \]

▼
The Kadison–Schwarz gap is positive semidefinite by Theorem .37. Trace preservation and the relation \(E(X)=\mu X\) with \(|\mu |=1\) show that its trace is zero, hence the gap vanishes.
▼

Theorem .42 Left multiplicative identity from KS equality

KadisonSchwarz.multiplicative_domain_left thm:kraus_commute_channels Let \(E\) be a unital Kraus map. If \(E(X^\dagger X) = E(X)^\dagger E(X)\), then for every \(Y \in M_{D}(\mathbb {C})\),

\[ E(X^\dagger Y) = E(X)^\dagger E(Y). \]

▼
thm:kraus_commute_channels The commutation relation of Theorem .40 lets one move \(X^\dagger \) past each Kraus operator inside the sum.
▼

Definition .43 Multiplicative domain

KadisonSchwarz.multiplicativeDomain def:kraus_map_channels The multiplicative domain of a Kraus map \(E\) is the set of matrices that are simultaneously left- and right-multiplicative for \(E\).

Theorem .44 Multiplicative domain is a \(*\)-subalgebra

KadisonSchwarz.multiplicativeDomainStarSubalgebra def:multiplicative_domain_channels, def:kraus_map_channels For a unital Kraus map, the multiplicative domain is a \(*\)-subalgebra of \(M_{D}(\mathbb {C})\).

▼
The multiplicative identities are stable under addition, multiplication, and adjoint, and they contain the identity matrix.
▼

Peripheral closure via adjoint fixed point

The following results replace the trace-preservation hypothesis by the existence of a positive definite fixed point for the adjoint Kraus map, matching the weighted-trace argument in [ CPGSV17a , Appendix A ] .

Lemma .45 Peripheral closure via adjoint fixed point

MPSTensor.peripheralEigenvalues_pow_mem_of_irreducible_unital_of_adjoint_fixedPoint def:kraus_map_channels, def:irreducible_cp Let \(E(X) = \sum _i K_i X K_i^\dagger \) be a Kraus map that is unital (\(\sum _i K_i K_i^\dagger = \mathbb {1}\)), and assume:

  1. the adjoint Kraus map \(E^*(X) = \sum _i K_i^\dagger X K_i\) has a positive definite fixed point \(\rho {\gt} 0\), and

  2. \(E\) is irreducible.

Then \(\mathrm{peripheral}(E)\) is closed under powers: if \(\mu \in \mathrm{peripheral}(E)\) then \(\mu ^n \in \mathrm{peripheral}(E)\) for every \(n \in \mathbb {N}\).

▼
thm:kadison_schwarz_channels, thm:psd_fp_pd_irred, thm:kraus_commute_channels, thm:left_multiplicative_identity_channels Let \(E(X) = \mu X\) with \(|\mu | = 1\), and set \(G := E(X^\dagger X) - E(X)^\dagger E(X)\). By Kadison–Schwarz, \(G \ge 0\). Since \(E^*(\rho ) = \rho \),

\[ \tr (\rho G) = \tr (\rho E(X^\dagger X)) - \tr (\rho E(X)^\dagger E(X)) = \tr (E^*(\rho ) X^\dagger X) - \tr (\rho E(X)^\dagger E(X)) = \tr (\rho X^\dagger X) - |\mu |^2 \tr (\rho X^\dagger X) = 0. \]

Thus the trace of the Kadison–Schwarz gap against the adjoint fixed point vanishes. Because \(\rho {\gt} 0\) and \(G \ge 0\), this forces \(G = 0\), so \(E(X^\dagger X) = E(X)^\dagger E(X)\). Hence \(X^\dagger X\) is a PSD fixed point, which is positive definite by irreducibility (Theorem .4). So \(X\) is invertible. By Theorem .40 (KS equality implies Kraus commutation), \(X\) commutes with each Kraus operator; iterating via the left multiplicative identity (Theorem .42) gives \(E(X^n) = \mu ^n X^n\), so \(\mu ^n\) is peripheral.

▼

Theorem .46 Roots of unity via adjoint fixed point

MPSTensor.peripheral_isRootOfUnity_of_irreducible_unital_of_adjoint_fixedPoint def:kraus_map_channels, def:irreducible_cp, def:peripheral_eigenvalues Let \(E(X) = \sum _i K_i X K_i^\dagger \) be a Kraus map that is unital, and assume that the adjoint Kraus map \(E^*(X) = \sum _i K_i^\dagger X K_i\) has a positive definite fixed point and that \(E\) is irreducible. Then every peripheral eigenvalue of \(E\) is a root of unity.

▼
lem:peripheral_closed_powers_irred_fp, thm:peripheral_powers Combine the closure-under-powers result (Lemma .45) with Theorem .31.
▼

Remark .47

Lemma .45 and Theorem .46 cover the bi-canonical (unital + TP) case as well: trace preservation gives \(E^*(\mathbb {1}) = \mathbb {1}\), which is a positive definite fixed point of the adjoint. The adjoint-fixed-point formulation matches the argument in [ CPGSV17a , Appendix A ] , which uses a faithful invariant state rather than bi-canonicality.

Definition .48 Channel period

channelPeriod def:peripheral_eigenvalues The period of a channel \(E\) is the number of peripheral eigenvalues (counted without multiplicity): \(p(E) := |\mathrm{peripheral}(E)|\).

Definition .49 Primitive map

IsPrimitive def:peripheral_eigenvalues A linear map is primitive if its only peripheral eigenvalue is \(\lambda = 1\), i.e. \(\mathrm{peripheral}(E) = \{ 1\} \). For channels this corresponds to [ Wol12 , Theorem 6.7 ] . This definition applies to any linear endomorphism, not only to channels.

Theorem .50 Primitivity equals period one

isPrimitive_iff_period_one def:primitive_channel, def:channel_period A linear map with a nonzero fixed point is primitive if and only if its period is \(1\).

▼
thm:one_peripheral If \(\mathrm{peripheral}(E) = \{ 1\} \), then \(|\mathrm{peripheral}(E)| = 1\). Conversely, if \(|\mathrm{peripheral}(E)| = 1\), then since \(1 \in \mathrm{peripheral}(E)\) (Theorem .27), the only element is \(1\).
▼

Theorem .51 Complementary transfer-map gap for primitive maps

compl_eigenvalue_norm_lt_one_of_primitive def:primitive_channel, def:fixed_point_proj, def:trace_preserving Let \(E : M_{D}(\mathbb {C}) \to M_{D}(\mathbb {C})\) be trace-preserving, and let \(\rho \neq 0\) satisfy \(E(\rho ) = \rho \) and \(\tr (\rho ) \neq 0\). Let

\[ P(X) = \frac{\tr (X)}{\tr (\rho )} \rho \]

be the fixed-point projection associated to \(\rho \). Assume that:

  1. \(E\) is primitive;

  2. every eigenvalue \(\mu \) of \(E\) satisfies \(|\mu | \le 1\);

  3. whenever \(E(X) = X\) and \(\tr (X) = 0\), one has \(X = 0\).

Then every eigenvalue \(\nu \) of \(E - P\) satisfies \(|\nu | {\lt} 1\).

▼
Let \((E - P)(X) = \nu X\) with \(X \neq 0\). Then

\[ E(X) = \nu X + P(X). \]

If \(\tr (X) = 0\), then \(P(X) = 0\), so \(E(X) = \nu X\) and hence \(\nu \) is an eigenvalue of \(E\) with \(|\nu | \le 1\). If \(|\nu | = 1\), primitivity forces \(\nu = 1\), so \(X\) is a trace-zero fixed point of \(E\); by assumption this implies \(X = 0\), a contradiction. Thus \(|\nu | {\lt} 1\).

If instead \(\tr (X) \neq 0\), trace preservation and \(\tr (P(X)) = \tr (X)\) give

\[ \tr (X) = \tr (E(X)) = \nu \tr (X) + \tr (P(X)) = \nu \tr (X) + \tr (X), \]

so \(\nu \tr (X) = 0\) and therefore \(\nu = 0\). In either case, \(|\nu | {\lt} 1\).

▼

Fixed-point algebra

Definition .52 Kraus fixed points

Kraus.fixedPointsdef:kraus_map_channels The fixed-point set of a Kraus map \(E(X) = \sum _i K_i X K_i^\dagger \) is \(\mathrm{Fix}(E) = \{ X \in M_{D}(\mathbb {C}) : E(X) = X\} \).

Theorem .53 Fixed points lie in the multiplicative domain

Kraus.fixedPoints_in_multiplicativeDomain def:kraus_fixed_points, def:multiplicative_domain_channels, def:kraus_map_channels Let \(E\) be a trace-preserving Kraus map whose Schrödinger-picture map has a positive definite fixed point \(\rho {\gt} 0\). Then every adjoint fixed point belongs to the multiplicative domain of the adjoint Kraus family.

▼
The Kadison–Schwarz equality for fixed points forces both one-sided multiplicative identities, so the fixed point lies in the full multiplicative domain.
▼

Theorem .54 Fixed points form a \(*\)-subalgebra

Kraus.fixedPointsStarSubalgebradef:kraus_fixed_points, def:kraus_map_channels Let \(E\) be a unital Kraus map whose adjoint map \(E^*\) has a positive definite fixed point \(\rho {\gt} 0\). Then \(\mathrm{Fix}(E)\) is a \(*\)-subalgebra of \(M_{D}(\mathbb {C})\). This is [ Wol12 , Theorem 6.12 ] .

▼
thm:kadison_schwarz_channels, thm:left_multiplicative_identity_channels Closure under addition and scalar multiplication is immediate from linearity. Closure under \(\dagger \): if \(E(X) = X\) then \(E(X^\dagger ) = X^\dagger \) because fixed points are stable under adjoint. Closure under multiplication: if \(E(X) = X\) and \(E(Y) = Y\), the weighted Kadison–Schwarz argument gives \(E(X^\dagger X) = E(X)^\dagger E(X)\) and, applied to \(X^\dagger \), also \(E(X X^\dagger ) = E(X) E(X)^\dagger \). Thus \(X\) lies in the multiplicative domain, and Theorem .42 yields \(E(XY) = E(X) E(Y) = XY\).
▼

Remark .55

Theorem .53 is the trace-preserving / Heisenberg-picture counterpart of the same multiplicative-domain argument.

Theorem .56 Projected elements lie in the multiplicative domain

Kraus.map_mem_multiplicativeDomain_of_idempotentdef:kraus_fixed_points, def:unital_kraus Let \(E\) be a unital Kraus map whose adjoint map has a positive definite fixed point, and suppose \(E^2 = E\). Then \(E(X)\) lies in the multiplicative domain of \(E\) for every \(X \in M_{D}(\mathbb {C})\).

▼
thm:fixedPointsStarSubalgebra, thm:md_characterization Idempotence gives \(E(E(X)) = E(X)\), so \(E(X)\) is a fixed point. By the argument in Theorem .54, the Kadison–Schwarz gap for the fixed point \(E(X)\) vanishes, hence

\[ E(E(X)^\dagger E(X)) = E(X)^\dagger E(X). \]

Fixed points are closed under \(\dagger \), so the same argument applied to \(E(X)^\dagger \) gives

\[ E(E(X) E(X)^\dagger ) = E(X) E(X)^\dagger . \]

Therefore Theorem .28 places \(E(X)\) in the multiplicative domain.

▼

Theorem .57 Choi–Effros left absorption

Kraus.choi_effros_leftdef:kraus_fixed_points, def:unital_kraus Under the same hypotheses, for all \(X, Y \in M_{D}(\mathbb {C})\),

\[ E(E(X) Y) = E(E(X) E(Y)). \]

▼
thm:map_mem_multiplicativeDomain_of_idempotent The previous theorem places \(E(X)\) in the multiplicative domain, so \(E(E(X) Y) = E(E(X)) E(Y) = E(X) E(Y)\). Applying the same multiplicativity to \(E(X)\) and \(E(Y)\) gives \(E(E(X) E(Y)) = E(E(X)) E(E(Y))\), and idempotence rewrites this as \(E(X) E(Y)\).
▼

Theorem .58 Choi–Effros right absorption

Kraus.choi_effros_rightdef:kraus_fixed_points, def:unital_kraus Under the same hypotheses, for all \(X, Y \in M_{D}(\mathbb {C})\),

\[ E(X E(Y)) = E(E(X) E(Y)). \]

▼
thm:map_mem_multiplicativeDomain_of_idempotent The previous theorem places \(E(Y)\) in the multiplicative domain, so \(E(X E(Y)) = E(X) E(E(Y)) = E(X) E(Y)\). Applying the same multiplicativity to \(E(X)\) and \(E(Y)\) gives \(E(E(X) E(Y)) = E(E(X)) E(E(Y))\), and idempotence rewrites this as \(E(X) E(Y)\).
▼

Theorem .59 Choi–Effros symmetric absorption

Kraus.choi_effros_left_eq_rightdef:kraus_fixed_points, def:unital_kraus Under the same hypotheses, for all \(X, Y \in M_{D}(\mathbb {C})\),

\[ E(E(X) Y) = E(X E(Y)). \]

▼
thm:choi_effros_left, thm:choi_effros_right Both sides equal \(E(E(X) E(Y))\) by the two preceding theorems.
▼

Theorem .60 Choi–Effros projected product

Kraus.choi_effros_sandwichdef:kraus_fixed_points, def:unital_kraus Under the same hypotheses, for all \(X, Y \in M_{D}(\mathbb {C})\),

\[ E(E(X) E(Y)) = E(X) E(Y). \]

▼
thm:map_mem_multiplicativeDomain_of_idempotent The previous theorem places \(E(X)\) and \(E(Y)\) in the multiplicative domain. Therefore multiplicativity gives

\[ E(E(X) E(Y)) = E(E(X)) E(E(Y)). \]

Since \(E\) is idempotent under the standing hypotheses, \(E(E(X)) = E(X)\) and \(E(E(Y)) = E(Y)\), so

\[ E(E(X) E(Y)) = E(X) E(Y). \]

▼

Definition .61 Adjoint fixed points

Kraus.adjointFixedPointsdef:kraus_map_channels The adjoint fixed-point set \(\mathrm{Fix}(E^*) = \{ X \in M_{D}(\mathbb {C}) : E^*(X) = X\} \), where \(E^*(X) = \sum _i K_i^\dagger X K_i\).

Theorem .62 Fixed points form a \(*\)-subalgebra in the Heisenberg picture

Kraus.adjointFixedPointsStarSubalgebra, Kraus.fixedPoints_starSubalgebradef:adjoint_fixed_points, def:kraus_map_channels If the Kraus map is trace-preserving and has a positive definite fixed point \(\rho {\gt} 0\) with \(E(\rho ) = \rho \), then \(\mathrm{Fix}(E^*)\) is a \(*\)-subalgebra of \(M_{D}(\mathbb {C})\).

▼
thm:fixedPointsStarSubalgebra Apply Theorem .54 to the adjoint family \(\{ K_i^\dagger \} \).
▼

Definition .63 Kraus commutant

Kraus.krausCommutant, Kraus.krausCommutantStarSubalgebradef:kraus_map_channels The Kraus commutant is \(\{ K_i, K_i^\dagger \} ' = \{ X \in M_{D}(\mathbb {C}) : [X, K_i] = [X, K_i^\dagger ] = 0 \forall i\} \). It is a \(*\)-subalgebra of \(M_{D}(\mathbb {C})\).

Theorem .64 Adjoint fixed points equal the Kraus commutant

Kraus.adjointFixedPointsStarSubalgebra_eq_krausCommutantStarSubalgebradef:adjoint_fixed_points, def:kraus_commutant, def:kraus_map_channels Under the hypotheses of Theorem .62, the adjoint fixed-point \(*\)-subalgebra equals the Kraus commutant:

\[ \mathrm{Fix}(E^*) = \{ K_i, K_i^\dagger \} '. \]

This is [ Wol12 , Theorem 6.13 ] .

▼
thm:adjointFixedPointsStarSubalgebra (\(\supseteq \)): if \(X\) commutes with all \(K_i\) and \(K_i^\dagger \), then \(E^*(X) = \sum _i K_i^\dagger X K_i = X \sum _i K_i^\dagger K_i = X\) by trace preservation. (\(\subseteq \)): any \(*\)-subalgebra inside \(\mathrm{Fix}(E^*)\) lies in the Kraus commutant, because for \(X \in \mathrm{Fix}(E^*)\) with \(X^\dagger X \in \mathrm{Fix}(E^*)\) the Kadison–Schwarz gap vanishes, which forces \(X K_i = K_i X\) and \(X K_i^\dagger = K_i^\dagger X\).
▼

Theorem .65 Kraus commutant inside the adjoint fixed points

Kraus.krausCommutantStarSubalgebra_isGreatest_adjointFixedPointStarSubalgebras thm:adjointFixedPoints_eq_krausCommutant Under the hypotheses above, the Kraus commutant is the largest \(*\)-subalgebra contained in the adjoint fixed-point set.

▼
By Theorem .64, every \(*\)-subalgebra inside the adjoint fixed points is automatically contained in the Kraus commutant, while the Kraus commutant itself is such a \(*\)-subalgebra.
▼
