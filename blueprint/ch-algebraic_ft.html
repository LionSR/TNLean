17 The Algebraic Fundamental Theorem

This chapter develops the one-dimensional MPS results of  : the Fundamental Theorem for injective and normal MPS on a finite periodic chain of fixed length. The argument is purely algebraic, working directly with \(M_{D}(\mathbb {C})\). Two preparatory lemmas—the virtual bond gauge (Lemma 17.11) and the proportionality lemma (Lemma 17.12)—carry the entire argument, yielding the main theorem and its corollaries for translation-invariant chains, non-translation-invariant descriptions of translation-invariant states, and blocked chains.

Throughout, \(D\) is the bond dimension and \(d\) is the physical dimension with \(d \ge D^2\). The extensions to injective and normal PEPS on arbitrary graphs and the improved system-size bound (\(2L+1\) in place of \(3L\)) are not pursued in this chapter. The chain data (Definition 4.12), including same chain state, injectivity, and cyclic gauge equivalence, was introduced in Chapter 4.

17.1 One-sided inverse and decomposition

If \(A\) is injective, the linear combination map \(\Phi _A(c) := \sum _{i} c_i A^i : \mathbb {C}^d \to M_{D}(\mathbb {C})\) is surjective and admits a right inverse.

17.2 Physical realisation of virtual insertions

For an injective tensor \(A\), the physical realisation map encodes the effect of inserting a virtual matrix \(X\) on the bond as a \(d \times d\) operation on the physical indices.

Definition 17.5 Physical realisation map

Let \(A\) be an injective tensor. For \(X \in M_{D}(\mathbb {C})\), define the \(d \times d\) matrix \(O_A(X) \in M_{d}(\mathbb {C})\) by

\[ O_A(X)_{ij} := (\Psi _A(A^i X))_j, \]

so that row \(i\) of \(O_A(X)\) contains the decomposition coefficients of \(A^i X\) in the spanning set \(\{ A^j\} \). Diagrammatically, the inserted virtual matrix is re-expressed as a physical operation on the same local tensor:

\[ \begin{array}{ccccccccc} \multicolumn{3}{c}{X} & & & \multicolumn{3}{c}{O_A(X)} \\ & & & & & & \vert & & \\ \bullet & \text{---} & \textcolor{red}{\bullet } & & = & & \bullet & & \\ & & & & & & \vert & & \\ & & & & & & \textcolor{red}{\bullet } & & \end{array} \]

The map \(X \mapsto O_A(X)\) is \(\mathbb {C}\)-linear.

Theorem 17.7 Physical realisation is multiplicative
✓

Let \(A\) be an injective MPS tensor. For all \(X, Y \in M_{D}(\mathbb {C})\),

\[ O_A(XY) = O_A(X)\, O_A(Y). \]
Proof ▶

Applying Lemma 17.6 to \(X\) yields

\[ A^i X = \sum _{j=0}^{d-1} O_A(X)_{ij}\, A^j. \]

Multiply by \(Y\) on the right and apply the linear map \(\Psi _A\):

\[ \Psi _A(A^i XY) = \sum _{j=0}^{d-1} O_A(X)_{ij}\, \Psi _A(A^j Y). \]

Taking the \(k\)th component of both sides gives

\[ O_A(XY)_{ik} = \sum _{j=0}^{d-1} O_A(X)_{ij}\, O_A(Y)_{jk} = (O_A(X)\, O_A(Y))_{ik}. \]

Since this holds for all \(i\) and \(k\), the matrices are equal.

Let \(A = (A_0, A_1, A_2)\) and \(B = (B_0, B_1, B_2)\) be two 3-site injective periodic chains with common bond dimension \(D \ge 1\) and \(\mathcal{V}(A) = \mathcal{V}(B)\) (same MPV family). Then at the middle bond there exists an invertible matrix \(Z \in \mathrm{GL}_D(\mathbb {C})\) such that the virtual-insertion trace coefficients agree after conjugation:

\[ \operatorname{tr}(A_0^{i_0} X\, A_1^{i_1} A_2^{i_2}) = \operatorname{tr}(B_0^{i_0} Z^{-1} X Z\, B_1^{i_1} B_2^{i_2}) \]

for all \(X \in M_{D}(\mathbb {C})\) and all physical indices \(i_0, i_1, i_2\).

In the paper  , Lemma 1 is stated for arbitrary chain length \(n \ge 3\) and any bond position, with uniqueness of \(Z\) up to scalar. The chain-level generalization to arbitrary \(n \ge 3\) and any bond position is not pursued here.

Proof ▶

The \(\mathcal{V}(A) = \mathcal{V}(B)\) hypothesis gives, for every \(X \in M_{D}(\mathbb {C})\), equality of the virtual-insertion trace coefficients: \(\operatorname{tr}(A_0^{i_0} X\, A_1^{i_1} A_2^{i_2}) = \operatorname{tr}(B_0^{i_0} Y\, B_1^{i_1} B_2^{i_2})\) for some \(Y\) depending on \(X\). Since all three site tensors on each side are injective, the physical realisation maps \(O_A, O_B\) are algebra homomorphisms by Theorem 17.7. Setting \(T(X) = Y\) defines a \(\mathbb {C}\)-linear multiplicative map, hence a nonzero \(\mathbb {C}\)-algebra endomorphism of \(M_{D}(\mathbb {C})\), which is bijective by Theorem 4.8. Skolem–Noether (Theorem 4.9) gives \(Z \in \mathrm{GL}_D(\mathbb {C})\) with \(T(X) = Z^{-1} X Z\).

17.8 Product algebra construction and per-block gauge

17.10 Block separation from canonical form

The multi-block Fundamental Theorem (Theorem 17.36) assumes per-block \(\mathcal{V}(A_k) = \mathcal{V}(B_k)\). In practice, one starts from the weaker global hypothesis that the block-diagonal tensors satisfy the same MPV family: \(\mathcal{V}\! (\bigoplus _k \mu _k A_k) = \mathcal{V}\! \[ \sum _k \mu _k^N V^{(N)}(A_k)_\sigma = \sum _k \mu _k^N V^{(N)}(B_k)_\sigma , \]

which is then combined with overlap estimates and the leading-block peel.

17.12.1 Global symmetry via gauge composition

The virtual representation theorem (Theorem 18.14) relies on the fact that composing two gauge transformations yields another gauge up to a scalar factor.

Lemma 17.52 Gauge ratio commutes with tensor matrices
✓
#

Uses

  • Definition 2.10

  • MPSTensor.gauge_ratio_commutes

Diagrammatically, the hypothesis is that the two local gauges

\[ \begin{array}{ccccc}& & i & & \\ & & \vert & & \\ \textcolor{red}{\bullet } & \text{---} & \bullet & \text{---} & \textcolor{red}{\bullet } \\ X & & & & X^{-1} \end{array} \qquad \text{and}\qquad \begin{array}{ccccc}& & i & & \\ & & \vert & & \\ \textcolor{red}{\bullet } & \text{---} & \bullet & \text{---} & \textcolor{red}{\bullet } \\ Y & & & & Y^{-1} \end{array} \]

Proof ▶

Theorem 17.54 Projective multiplication law for gauge matrices
✓
#

Uses

  • Lemma 17.53
  • Definition 2.21
  • Definition 18.4
  • Lemma 18.5
  • Lemma 17.53
  • Lemma 18.5

Let \(A\) be an injective MPS tensor with on-site symmetry under a group representation \(U : G \to \mathrm{GL}_d(\mathbb {C})\). For each \(g \in G\), let \(X(g) \in \mathrm{GL}_D(\mathbb {C})\) satisfy \(\widetilde{A}_g^i = X(g)\, A^i\, X(g)^{-1}\) for all \(i\). Then for all \(g, h \in G\), there exists \(c(g,h) \in \mathbb {C}\) such that

\[ X(h)\, X(g) = c(g,h)\, X(g \cdot h). \]

\[ \begin{array}{ccccccccccc}& & i & & & & & & i & & \\ & & \vert & & & & & & \vert & & \\ \bullet & \text{---} & \textcolor{red}{\bullet } & \text{---} & \bullet & = & \bullet & \text{---} & \textcolor{red}{\bullet } & \text{---} & \bullet \\ & & X(h)\, X(g) & & & & & & X(gh) & & \end{array} \]

and both sides conjugate \(A^i\) to the same twisted tensor \(\widetilde{A}_{gh}^i\).
